Knowing you should eat more protein is one thing. Actually doing it, day after day, is another. The good news is that you do not need shakes, special diets or a complete kitchen overhaul. A handful of simple habits will take most people from falling short to comfortably hitting their target.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>Add protein to breakfast, include a protein source at every meal, keep high-protein snacks on hand, and make easy swaps like Greek yogurt for cream. Small additions across the day add up fast.</div>

## First, know your target

Before adding protein everywhere, it helps to know how much you actually need. Most active people do well on **1.6 to 2.0 g per kilo** of body weight, while sedentary adults need around 0.8 g. Our [protein calculator](/calculators/protein/) gives you a personalised number, and our full guide on [how much protein you need](/blog/how-much-protein-per-day/) explains the reasoning.

Once you have a number, the goal is simply to spread it across the day.

## Fix breakfast first

Breakfast is where most people lose the most ground. A typical bowl of cereal or slice of toast offers around 5 g of protein, while lunch and dinner usually do fine. Fixing breakfast alone can add 20 g or more.

- Swap cereal for **eggs** or a **veggie omelette**
- Stir protein powder into **overnight oats**
- Blend a **protein smoothie** with milk, yogurt and fruit
- Top wholegrain toast with **cottage cheese** or eggs

Our [banana protein pancakes](/recipes/banana-protein-pancakes/) and [protein berry smoothie](/recipes/protein-berry-smoothie/) are easy ways to start the day at 20 to 30 g.

## Include protein at every meal

Make a protein source the anchor of each plate, then build around it. Chicken, fish, eggs, dairy, beans, lentils and tofu all work. If a meal has no obvious protein, that is the one to fix.

| Add this | Protein boost |
| --- | --- |
| One egg | 6 to 7 g |
| Spoon of Greek yogurt | 5 g |
| Handful of beans or lentils | 7 to 9 g |
| 30 g grated cheese | 7 g |
| Scoop of cottage cheese | 11 g |
| Small tin of tuna | 16 g |

## Make simple swaps

You can lift the protein of meals you already eat without cooking anything new:

- **Greek yogurt instead of cream** in sauces and on desserts
- **Wholegrain or higher-protein pasta** in place of white
- **Extra beans or lentils** stirred into soups, stews and salads
- **Chicken or tofu** added to a vegetable stir-fry or rice bowl

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>Protein swaps beat protein additions.</strong> Replacing a low-protein food with a high-protein one raises your intake without adding extra calories, which is ideal if you are also watching your weight.</p></div>

## Keep high-protein snacks ready

Snacks are an easy place to add 25 to 40 g across the day. Keep Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, boiled eggs, edamame or jerky on hand. Our guide to [high-protein snacks](/blog/high-protein-snacks/) has 20 ideas, and the [high-protein foods list](/food-lists/high-protein-foods/) shows the best sources by category.

## Use protein powder as backup, not the plan

There is nothing wrong with a protein shake, especially around training or on a rushed morning. But treat it as a convenient backup for the days whole foods fall short, not the foundation of your intake. Food brings other nutrients, fibre and fullness that powder alone does not.

## The bottom line

Eating more protein is less about effort and more about a few reliable habits: fix breakfast, anchor every meal with protein, swap rather than add where you can, and keep good snacks ready.
