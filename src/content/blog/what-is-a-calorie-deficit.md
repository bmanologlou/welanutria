Every diet that has ever worked, low-carb, high-protein, intermittent fasting, the lot, works for the same reason underneath: a calorie deficit. Understand this one idea and you can stop chasing diet trends and focus on what actually drives fat loss.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>A calorie deficit means eating less energy than you burn, so your body uses stored fat to fill the gap. Aim for 300 to 500 calories below your maintenance level for steady, sustainable fat loss.</div>

## What a calorie deficit is

Your body needs a certain amount of energy each day to run, your maintenance level, or [TDEE](/calculators/tdee/). A **calorie deficit** is simply taking in less than that.

When you do, your body has to find the missing energy somewhere, and it draws on its reserves, mostly body fat. That's fat loss, in one sentence. Every successful diet creates this gap; they just go about it in different ways.

## Why it's the only thing that matters for fat loss

This is the part that cuts through the noise. Carbs don't make you fat. Eating after 8pm doesn't make you fat. What matters is the **net balance** between energy in and energy out. Diets that cut carbs or restrict eating windows work because they help you eat fewer calories overall, not because of magic.

That's liberating: you don't need a special diet, just a sustainable way to land below your maintenance.

## How big should your deficit be?

For most people, **300 to 500 calories a day** below maintenance is the sweet spot, enough for steady progress, gentle enough to sustain.

| Deficit per day | Roughly per week | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 250 kcal | 0.25 kg | Slow, very sustainable loss |
| 500 kcal | 0.5 kg | The standard for most people |
| 750 to 1,000 kcal | 0.75 to 1 kg | Faster loss, harder to sustain |

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>Bigger isn't better.</strong> Large deficits lose weight faster but bring more hunger, more muscle loss and a much higher chance of quitting. A deficit you can actually live with wins every time.</p></div>

## Three ways to create one

You have three levers, and the best results usually come from combining them gently rather than maxing out any single one:

- **Eat a bit less.** Smaller portions, fewer liquid calories, more filling foods.
- **Move a bit more.** Walking, steps and general activity add up. [Exercise](/calculators/calories-burned/) burns extra on top.
- **Eat more protein and fibre.** These keep you full, so the deficit feels smaller than it is.

## Why deficits stall (and what to do)

Weight loss rarely follows a neat straight line. As you get lighter, you burn slightly fewer calories, so the same intake creates a smaller deficit over time. Water retention can also mask fat loss on the scale for days.

If progress stalls for three to four weeks despite consistency, trim another 100 to 200 calories or add more daily movement, rather than slashing your intake dramatically.
