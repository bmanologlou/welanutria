"Anti-inflammatory eating" is one of the biggest nutrition themes right now, and for once the hype has real substance behind it. The catch is that it is not about a few magic foods. It is about a whole pattern of eating. Here is what to include, what to limit, and why the bigger picture matters more than any single ingredient.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>An anti-inflammatory diet is built on whole foods: oily fish, berries, leafy greens, nuts, seeds, olive oil and spices like ginger and turmeric, while limiting ultra-processed foods, refined carbs and added sugar. The overall pattern matters far more than any single food.</div>

## What "inflammation" actually means here

Short-term inflammation is your body's healthy response to injury or infection. The concern is **chronic, low-grade inflammation**, which is linked to many long-term health issues. Diet is one of several factors (alongside sleep, exercise, stress and smoking) that influence it. No food switches inflammation on or off by itself, but the overall way you eat genuinely moves the needle.

These are the foods that appear again and again in anti-inflammatory eating patterns:

| Nuts & seeds | Walnuts, flax, chia | Omega-3, fibre |

## What to limit

The other half of the picture is easing off the foods linked to inflammation:

- **Ultra-processed foods** (packaged snacks, ready meals high in additives)
- **Refined carbohydrates** (white bread, pastries, many cereals)
- **Added sugar and sugary drinks**
- **Large amounts of processed meat**

You do not need to be perfect. The goal is to shift the balance of your plate towards whole foods, most of the time.

## The Mediterranean connection

The best-studied anti-inflammatory pattern is essentially the **Mediterranean diet**: olive oil, fish, vegetables, fruit, legumes, nuts and whole grains, with less red and processed meat. If "anti-inflammatory diet" feels vague, following a Mediterranean-style way of eating is the most practical, evidence-backed version.

## Putting it into practice

- Build meals around **vegetables, whole grains and a protein source**
- Eat **oily fish** once or twice a week, or plant omega-3 (flax, chia, walnuts) daily
- Use **extra virgin olive oil** as your main fat
- Snack on **berries, nuts and plain yogurt** rather than packaged snacks
- Add **ginger and turmeric** to cooking for flavour and variety

## The bottom line

Anti-inflammatory eating is not a strict diet or a detox. It is a sustainable, whole-food pattern, close to the Mediterranean way of eating, that supports long-term health. Focus on adding more whole foods rather than obsessing over a single ingredient, and let the overall pattern do the work.
