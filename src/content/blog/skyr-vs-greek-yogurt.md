Skyr and Greek yogurt sit side by side in the fridge, look almost identical, and both promise a protein hit. So what actually separates them, and can you swap one for the other? Here is the straightforward answer, with the real numbers.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>Skyr and Greek yogurt are very similar high-protein dairy foods and you can substitute one for the other freely. Skyr is technically a fresh cheese and is marginally higher in protein (around 11g vs 10g per 100g) and calcium (around 150mg vs 110mg). For most people, the best choice is simply whichever you prefer.</div>

## What is the actual difference?

The difference is mostly in how they are made:

- **Skyr** is a traditional **Icelandic** product, made more like a soft fresh cheese. Milk is cultured, then strained to remove whey, leaving a thick, mild result.
- **Greek yogurt** is **yogurt** that has been strained to remove whey, giving that same thick texture.

In other words, skyr is technically a cheese and Greek yogurt is a yogurt, but in taste, texture and everyday use they are almost the same. That is why they are so easily confused.

## The numbers: skyr vs Greek yogurt

Here is how the plain, fat-free versions compare per 100g:

| Per 100g | Skyr | Greek yogurt (0% fat) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Protein | ~11g | ~10g |
| Calories | ~63 | ~59 |
| Calcium | ~150mg | ~110mg |
| Fat | Very low | Very low |

As you can see, the differences are small. **Skyr edges ahead slightly on protein and calcium**, but both are excellent, lean, high-protein choices. You can compare them side by side on our [Greek yogurt vs skyr comparison](/compare/greek-yogurt-vs-skyr/).

## Can you substitute one for the other?

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>Yes, freely.</strong> In smoothies, breakfast bowls, dips, sauces, baking and as a topping, skyr and Greek yogurt work the same way. Their similar thickness, protein and fat mean you can swap them one for one without changing your recipe.</p></div>

This is the most common question about these two, and the answer is simple: use whichever you have. The small nutritional differences will not noticeably affect a recipe.

## Which should you choose?

Since they are so similar, base your choice on practical things:

- **Choose skyr** for a slightly higher protein and calcium hit, if you can find it.
- **Choose Greek yogurt** for wider availability and more flavour varieties.
- **Either way, go plain.** Flavoured versions of both can carry a lot of added sugar. Add your own fruit or a drizzle of honey instead.

Skyr and Greek yogurt are close cousins: both thick, both high in protein, both low in fat.
