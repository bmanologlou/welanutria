If you lift weights, your one-rep max is one of the most useful numbers you can know. It tells you where your strength stands and lets you program your training precisely. The good news: you don't have to risk a true max attempt to find it.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>Your one-rep max (1RM) is the most you can lift once. You can estimate it safely from a normal set using the Epley formula, no maximal attempt needed, and use it to set training weights as percentages.</div>

## What a one-rep max is

Your **one-rep max**, or 1RM, is the heaviest weight you can lift for a single clean repetition of an exercise. It's the standard benchmark for strength: when someone asks "what do you bench?", they usually mean your 1RM.

Beyond bragging rights, it's genuinely useful, because most structured strength programs set their loads relative to it.

## Why you shouldn't always test it directly

You *could* find your 1RM by loading the bar heavier and heavier until you can only manage one rep. But a true maximal attempt carries real **injury risk**, especially without a spotter or solid technique, and it's taxing to recover from.

That's why estimating is so popular. You take a set you can already do for several reps and use a formula to project what your single-rep max would be.

## How the estimate works

The most widely used method is the **Epley formula**:

> 1RM ≈ weight × (1 + reps ÷ 30)

So if you lift 100 kg for 5 reps, your estimated 1RM is about 117 kg. Our [one-rep max calculator](/calculators/one-rep-max/) does this instantly and builds a full percentage table.

The estimate is most reliable at **lower rep counts**, around 10 reps or fewer. The further you go beyond that, the more the projection drifts, because very high-rep sets depend heavily on endurance rather than pure strength.

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>Use a challenging but clean set.</strong> For the best estimate, pick a set of around 3 to 8 reps where the last rep was tough but your form held. Junk reps with poor technique throw the number off.</p></div>

## Training with percentages

Here's where your 1RM earns its keep. Strength programs commonly prescribe work as a **percentage of your 1RM**, because it scales the load to your current strength.

| % of 1RM | Typical use | Approx reps |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 90 to 100% | Peak strength | 1 to 3 |
| 80 to 90% | Strength | 3 to 6 |
| 70 to 80% | Strength and size | 6 to 10 |
| 60 to 70% | Size and volume | 10 to 15 |

Knowing your 1RM turns "do 5 reps at 80 percent" into an actual number on the bar.

## Don't forget recovery

Strength is built between sessions, not just during them, and that needs adequate [protein](/calculators/protein/) and rest. Lifting heavy without fuelling and recovering properly is a fast route to a plateau.

## Putting it together

1. **Pick a solid set** of around 3 to 8 clean reps.
2. **Estimate your 1RM** with the [one-rep max calculator](/calculators/one-rep-max/).
3. **Use the percentage table** to set your training weights.
4. **Fuel and recover** so the strength actually shows up.

Estimating your 1RM gives you the precision of structured training without the risk of a max attempt, the best of both worlds.
