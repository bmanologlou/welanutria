Gut health has gone from a niche topic to one of the biggest themes in nutrition, and for good reason. The trillions of bacteria in your gut influence digestion, immunity, mood and more. The encouraging part is that you can shape that microbiome with everyday food, no expensive supplements required.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>Eat more fibre-rich plants and some fermented foods. Fibre feeds your good bacteria, fermented foods add live cultures, and variety matters most. A simple guide is the three F's: fibre, fermented foods and fluids.</div>

## How food shapes your gut

Your gut microbiome is the community of bacteria living in your digestive system. When it is diverse and balanced, it helps you digest food, produces useful compounds and supports your immune system. What you eat is the biggest lever you have over it.

Two types of food matter most, and they work as a pair.

- **Prebiotics** are types of fibre that feed your beneficial bacteria. They are found in high-fibre plant foods.
- **Probiotics** are live beneficial bacteria themselves, found in fermented foods.

Think of prebiotics as the food and probiotics as the guests. Bring both and the good bacteria thrive.

## Fibre: the foundation

Fibre is the single most important factor for gut health. Your beneficial bacteria ferment it to produce short-chain fatty acids that nourish the gut lining. The diets that produce the healthiest, most diverse microbiomes are simply the ones richest in fibre from a wide range of plants.

| Prebiotic (high-fibre) food | Also provides |
| --- | --- |
| Oats | Soluble fibre, slow energy |
| Bananas | Resistant starch, potassium |
| Onions and garlic | Natural prebiotic fibres |
| Beans and lentils | Fibre plus plant protein |
| Apples and berries | Fibre plus antioxidants |
| Leafy greens | Volume, vitamins, fibre |
| Nuts and seeds | Fibre plus healthy fats |

## Fermented foods: live cultures

Fermented foods add living bacteria directly to your gut. The best options include:

- **Yogurt and kefir** with live or active cultures
- **Sauerkraut** (unpasteurised)
- **Kimchi**
- **Miso and tempeh** (fermented soy)

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>Check the label for "live" or "active cultures".</strong> Heat and pasteurisation kill the beneficial bacteria, so many shelf-stable products do not contain live probiotics. Foods pickled in vinegar rather than salt usually do not either.</p></div>

A simple starting point is a daily serving of yogurt or kefir with live cultures. Our [Greek yogurt protein bowl](/recipes/greek-yogurt-protein-bowl/) is an easy way to work it into breakfast.

## The three F's

If you want one easy framework, remember the three F's:

- **Fibre** from a wide variety of plants, the more colours and types the better
- **Fermented foods** for live cultures, a small daily serving
- **Fluids** to keep everything moving, mainly water

Alongside these, cutting back on ultra-processed foods and eating at fairly regular times both help your gut settle into a healthy rhythm.

## Variety is the real secret

Researchers consistently find that the single best predictor of a healthy gut is the **number of different plant foods** you eat, not any one superfood. A common target is around 30 different plants a week, counting vegetables, fruits, whole grains, beans, nuts, seeds and herbs. It sounds like a lot, but a varied bowl or mixed salad can include half a dozen at once.

## A note on supplements and bloating

For most healthy people, food beats supplements. Fermented foods bring a broader range of bacteria plus fibre and nutrients that pills lack. If you do consider a probiotic supplement, it is worth speaking to a healthcare professional first.

If adding fibre or fermented foods causes mild bloating at first, that is normal. Start small, increase gradually, and it usually settles within a week or two as your gut adjusts.

## The bottom line

A healthy gut does not need exotic products or expensive supplements. It needs fibre from a wide variety of plants, a little fermented food for live cultures, and enough water to keep things moving. Build meals around plants, add a daily serving of yogurt or another fermented food, and your microbiome does the rest.
