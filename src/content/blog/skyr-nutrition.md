Skyr has become one of the most popular high-protein dairy foods, and people increasingly want to know exactly what is in it, especially its calcium and protein content. Here is a clear breakdown of skyr's nutrition, its benefits, and how much of your daily needs a tub actually covers.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>Plain skyr is high in protein (~11g per 100g), a good source of calcium (~150mg per 100g), low in fat and low in calories (~63 per 100g). A 150g tub gives roughly 16g protein and 225mg calcium, making it excellent for muscle, bones and fullness.</div>

## Skyr nutrition at a glance

Here is what plain skyr provides, per 100 g and per typical 150 g tub:

| Nutrient | Per 100g | Per 150g tub |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Calories | ~63 | ~95 |
| Protein | ~11g | ~16g |
| Calcium | ~150mg | ~225mg |
| Potassium | ~150mg | ~225mg |
| Fat | ~0.2g | ~0.3g |
| Carbs | ~4g | ~6g |

## Calcium in skyr

Skyr is a **good source of calcium**, with around **150 mg per 100 g**, or about **225 mg in a 150 g tub**. For context, adults need roughly 700 to 1,000 mg of calcium a day, so a single tub provides around a fifth to a quarter of that.

That is slightly more calcium than most Greek yogurts (around 110 mg per 100 g), which makes skyr one of the higher-calcium options in the yogurt aisle. Calcium is essential for **bone health**, which matters at every age and becomes especially important later in life. See our [high-calcium foods list](/food-lists/high-calcium-foods/) for other good sources.

## Protein in skyr

Protein is skyr's headline feature: around **11 g per 100 g**, or roughly **16 g per tub**. That is exceptionally high for a dairy food and part of why skyr is so popular for muscle building, fullness and weight management.

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>Why the high protein matters:</strong> protein keeps you full for longer, helps preserve muscle, and has a higher "thermic effect" than carbs or fat. A tub of skyr as a snack can genuinely curb appetite.</p></div>

Work out how much protein you need each day with our [protein calculator](/calculators/protein/), and see our [high-protein foods list](/food-lists/high-protein-foods/) for more top sources.

## Potassium and other nutrients

Skyr provides around **150 mg of potassium per 100 g**, a modest contribution to your daily intake. It also contains small amounts of B12, zinc and folate, alongside its standout protein and calcium.

## Is skyr good for you?

For most people, plain skyr is an excellent choice:

- **High in protein** for muscle and fullness
- **Good source of calcium** for bones
- **Very low in fat** and **low in calories**
- **Versatile** in breakfasts, smoothies, baking and snacks

The main thing to watch is **added sugar** in flavoured versions. Choosing plain skyr and adding your own fruit keeps it as healthy as possible.

## Skyr vs Greek yogurt

Skyr and Greek yogurt are very similar, and you can [substitute one for the other](/blog/skyr-vs-greek-yogurt/) freely. Skyr is marginally higher in both protein and calcium, while Greek yogurt is more widely available. For a full side-by-side, see our [Greek yogurt vs skyr comparison](/compare/greek-yogurt-vs-skyr/).

## The bottom line

Skyr is a nutritional standout: very high in protein, a good source of calcium, low in fat and low in calories. A single 150 g tub delivers around 16 g of protein and 225 mg of calcium, supporting muscle, bones and fullness in one go. Stick to plain versions to keep added sugar down, and it is one of the best everyday dairy choices you can make.
