If you've ever tried to lose or gain weight, you've run into the same question: how many calories do you actually burn in a day? That number is your TDEE, and it's the single most useful figure for managing your weight. Here's what it means and how to find yours.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is the total calories you burn in a day. Eat around it to maintain weight, below it to lose, and above it to gain. It's your BMR multiplied by an activity factor.</div>

## What TDEE actually means

**TDEE** stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure. It's everything your body burns in 24 hours, added up: keeping your organs running, walking to the kitchen, typing, fidgeting, and any exercise you do.

Think of it as your daily calorie "budget". Spend exactly that and your weight holds steady. Spend less and you lose; spend more and you gain. Almost all weight management comes down to this one comparison.

## How TDEE is calculated

TDEE is built in two steps.

First, your **BMR** (Basal Metabolic Rate): the calories you'd burn lying in bed all day, doing nothing. This covers the basics like breathing, circulation and keeping your cells alive. It's usually the biggest chunk of your total, often 60 to 70 percent.

The most trusted way to estimate BMR is the **Mifflin-St Jeor equation**, which uses your sex, age, height and weight.

Second, that BMR is multiplied by an **activity factor** that reflects how much you move:

| Activity level | Multiplier |
| --- | --- |
| Sedentary (little exercise) | 1.2 |
| Lightly active (1 to 3 days/week) | 1.375 |
| Moderately active (3 to 5 days/week) | 1.55 |
| Very active (6 to 7 days/week) | 1.725 |
| Extremely active (hard daily training) | 1.9 |

BMR times your activity factor gives your TDEE. Our [TDEE calculator](/calculators/tdee/) does the whole thing for you, and adds a macro split on top.

## BMR vs TDEE: the difference

People mix these up, so to be clear:

- **BMR** is what you burn at complete rest.
- **TDEE** is BMR *plus* everything else you do.

Your TDEE is always higher than your BMR, even if you're sedentary, because you still move around a little during the day.

## How to use your TDEE

Once you have your number, the rest is simple:

- **To maintain weight:** eat around your TDEE.
- **To lose fat:** eat below it, a deficit of 300 to 500 calories suits most people. See our [calorie deficit calculator](/calculators/calorie-deficit/).
- **To build muscle:** eat slightly above it, a surplus of around 200 to 300 calories, paired with training.

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>It's a starting point, not gospel.</strong> Every TDEE estimate carries a margin of around 10 percent. Use your number for a few weeks, watch how your weight responds, and adjust up or down from there.</p></div>
