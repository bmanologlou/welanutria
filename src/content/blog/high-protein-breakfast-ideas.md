Breakfast is the meal where protein most often goes missing. Cereal, toast and pastries are quick but light on protein, leaving you hungry by mid-morning. Fixing that one meal is the single easiest way to feel fuller, snack less and hit your daily protein target.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>Aim for at least 20 g of protein at breakfast. Eggs, Greek yogurt, protein smoothies, cottage cheese and overnight oats with protein powder all get you there quickly and keep you full for hours.</div>

## Why breakfast protein matters most

Your body uses protein best in moderate amounts spread across the day, roughly 20 to 40 g per meal. Most people manage that at lunch and dinner but fall well short at breakfast, where a typical meal offers around 5 g.

Closing that gap does two things. It keeps you full through the morning, which cuts the snacking that quietly derails many diets, and it makes your daily total far easier to reach. Our [protein calculator](/calculators/protein/) shows what that daily target should be.

Under five minutes, no stove required.

| Cottage cheese on wholegrain toast | 22 g |
| Protein smoothie (milk, yogurt, powder) | 30 to 40 g |
| Chia pudding made with milk | 15 to 20 g |
| Overnight oats with protein powder | 25 to 30 g |

- **Avocado and egg toast.** Wholegrain toast, smashed avocado and two eggs, around 19 g. Try [avocado and egg toast](/recipes/avocado-egg-toast/).
- **Banana protein pancakes.** Made with protein powder and egg. See [banana protein pancakes](/recipes/banana-protein-pancakes/).
- **Egg white scramble.** Light, very high protein, low calorie. Try the [egg white and veggie scramble](/recipes/egg-white-veggie-scramble/).

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>Add one thing to hit 20 g.</strong> If your usual breakfast is light on protein, a single addition often closes the gap: a couple of eggs, a scoop of cottage cheese, a spoon of Greek yogurt, or a measure of protein powder in your oats.</p></div>

## How to build your own

You do not need recipes to get this right. Use a simple formula: pick a protein base, add fibre, and keep added sugar low.

- **Protein base:** eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, protein powder
- **Fibre:** oats, wholegrain toast, berries, seeds, fruit
- **Flavour:** cinnamon, vanilla, a little honey, nut butter

Combine one from each line and most breakfasts land at 20 g of protein or more.

## The bottom line

A high-protein breakfast is the highest-return change you can make to your daily eating. It keeps you full, curbs snacking and sets up your protein total for the day. Pick two or three options you enjoy, keep the ingredients on hand, and mornings sort themselves out.
