You've heard it a hundred times: drink eight glasses of water a day. It's simple, memorable, and not actually based on much. Your real water needs depend on your body and how you live. Here's a more useful way to think about it.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>The short answer</strong>Aim for roughly 30 to 35 ml of water per kilo of body weight, plus extra when you exercise. For most adults that's around 2 to 3 litres a day. Let thirst and urine colour fine-tune it.</div>

## Where the "8 glasses" rule falls down

The eight-glasses guideline isn't harmful, but it's arbitrary. It treats a 55 kg office worker and a 95 kg manual labourer as if they need exactly the same amount, which makes no sense. A bigger body and a more active day both mean more water lost and more to replace.

A better approach scales with **you**: your body weight and your activity level.

## How much you actually need

A practical estimate starts from your weight, roughly **30 to 35 ml per kilogram**, then adds an allowance for exercise. Our [water intake calculator](/calculators/water/) does exactly this and gives you a figure in litres and glasses.

Here's a rough idea by weight, before adding exercise:

| Body weight | Approx daily water |
| --- | --- |
| 55 kg | ~1.8 litres |
| 70 kg | ~2.3 litres |
| 85 kg | ~2.8 litres |
| 100 kg | ~3.3 litres |

Add to this on days you train, in hot weather, or when you're ill.

## Food and drink count too

Your daily water doesn't all have to come from a glass. Around **20 percent** typically comes from food, especially water-rich fruit and vegetables. Tea and coffee count as well; despite their reputation, the mild diuretic effect doesn't outweigh the fluid they provide.

So the figure from a calculator is a **total** target, not a plain-water quota.

<div class="highlight"><p><strong>The easiest hydration check.</strong> Glance at the colour of your urine. Pale yellow generally means you're well hydrated; dark yellow is a sign to drink more. It's simpler and more reliable than counting glasses.</p></div>

## Tips for staying hydrated

- **Spread it out.** Sip through the day rather than forcing large amounts at once.
- **Drink more when you sweat.** Hot days and hard [workouts](/calculators/calories-burned/) raise your needs.
- **Eat your water.** Fruit, vegetables and soups all contribute.
- **Keep a bottle handy.** Convenience does most of the work.

## Can you drink too much?

For most people, no, the body is good at handling extra fluid. Very rarely, drinking enormous amounts in a short time can dilute blood sodium dangerously, but that's uncommon and usually linked to extreme endurance events. Drinking to thirst and spreading intake out keeps you safely in the clear.

## Putting it together

1. **Estimate your target** with the [water calculator](/calculators/water/).
2. **Remember food counts** toward the total.
3. **Adjust for heat and exercise.**
4. **Check urine colour** as your simplest feedback.

Forget the rigid eight glasses. Scale your water to your body and your day, and let your thirst do the rest.
